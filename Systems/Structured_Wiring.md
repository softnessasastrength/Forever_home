# Structured Wiring and Multimedia Standard

## Purpose

The house should support current and future television, satellite, networking, audio, automation, and communications systems without requiring finished walls to be opened for routine upgrades.

The design is technology-agnostic. DIRECTV SWiM is a current reference case, not a permanent requirement.

## Central Low-Voltage Location

Provide a dedicated low-voltage / technology area in the finished basement near, but appropriately separated from, electrical and mechanical equipment.

The location should support:

- Wall-mounted or floor-standing network rack
- Fiber or cable internet demarcation and ONT/modem
- Router and firewall
- Ethernet patch panels
- Managed network switches
- UPS backup
- Coaxial distribution and grounding hardware
- Satellite multiswitch or SWiM equipment if installed
- Home automation controllers
- NAS or home server
- Future whole-home audio distribution
- Service lighting, ventilation, and working clearance

All structured wiring should home-run to this central location unless a later design decision documents a better topology.

## Multimedia Outlet Standard

Primary media locations should receive a coordinated multimedia outlet cluster rather than isolated jacks.

Baseline cluster:

- Two duplex 120 V receptacles or equivalent power capacity
- Two Ethernet ports
- One RG-6 coaxial port
- One oversized low-voltage box or brush plate for future cabling
- Empty conduit back to an accessible pathway or the technology room where practical

The exact count may be increased for the main living room, office, boutique, bedrooms, basement living area, and any dedicated media wall.

Power and low-voltage components must be separated and installed in accordance with applicable code.

## Ethernet

- Use Cat6A as the current conceptual baseline, or the best appropriate standard at construction time
- Minimum two data drops at primary multimedia locations
- Dedicated drops for office workstations, televisions, access points, cameras, printers, and fixed smart-home devices
- Home-run topology to patch panels
- Label both ends of every cable
- Maintain an as-built cable schedule and port map in the repository

## Coaxial and Satellite Readiness

Provide RG-6 or the appropriate current coaxial standard to primary media locations.

Satellite-ready planning should include:

- Roof or exterior mounting zone that does not conflict with the preferred solar roof plane
- Grounding and bonding pathway
- Weatherproof exterior entry point
- Conduit from the roof/exterior antenna zone to the technology room
- Coaxial home runs suitable for a DIRECTV SWiM system or equivalent distribution system
- Space and power for splitters, power inserters, multiswitches, or future replacement equipment

No satellite hardware should be placed where it unnecessarily shades solar panels or compromises roof drainage and maintenance.

## Wireless Network

- Provide hardwired ceiling locations for wireless access points
- Plan coverage by floor rather than relying on a single consumer router
- Include Ethernet and power-over-Ethernet capability at access-point locations
- Coordinate access points with decorative ceilings, lighting, and smoke detection

## Conduit and Pathways

Empty conduit is a core future-proofing requirement.

Preferred pathways:

- Technology room to attic
- Technology room to garage
- Technology room to main living-room media wall
- Technology room to office
- Technology room to boutique
- Technology room to basement living area
- Technology room to exterior service/demarcation area
- Technology room to roof or antenna mounting zone

Conduit should include pull strings, accessible bends, and documented endpoints. Oversized pathways are preferred where cost and structure allow.

## Documentation

The final project package should include:

- Low-voltage floor plans
- Outlet and jack schedules
- Cable labels
- Patch-panel map
- Rack elevation
- Conduit routing diagram
- Demarcation and grounding details
- As-built photographs before drywall

## Design Rule

No proprietary technology should be required for the house to function. The permanent investment is the pathway, power, space, grounding, and wiring infrastructure; individual devices may change over time.
